Mathematical Statements

Let us start with a statement, also known as a proposition. In math, a statement is a sentence or a mathematical expression that is either true or false.

    For example:
  • If a circle has a radius of r, its area is πr2 (true)
  • The moon is made of cheese (false)
  • Some triangles are isosceles (true)
  • 5 = 2 (false)
  • All triangles are isosceles (false)

There can also be open sentences where the truth depends on one or more variables.

    For example:
  • x + 1 = 2
  • The integer x is even
Logical Connectives

We can also combine statements using connectives such as “not”, “and”, “or”, “if… then”, and “if and only if”

Negation

To negate a statement, place the word “not” within the original statement.

The negation of the proposition p is denoted as ~p which reads “not p”

A statement and its negation have opposite truth values.

    For example:
  • p: There are seven days in a week.
  • ~p: There are not seven days in a week.
  • q: 9 + 10 = 21
  • ~q: 9 + 10 ≠ 21

Conjunction

The conjunction of proposition p and q is denoted as p⋀q, which reads “p and q”

A conjunction p⋀q is only true when both parts are true.

    For example:
  • p: Bote does not enjoy art.
  • q: Luke is a Reddit moderator.
  • p⋀q: Bote does not enjoy art and luke is a Reddit moderator.

Disjunction

The disjunction of two proposition p and q is denoted as p⋁q, which reads “p or q”

A disjunction p⋁q is true when any part of the compound statement is true.

    For example:
  • p: Omar has awesome hair.
  • q: Victor can wash pots.
  • p⋁q: Omar has awesome hair or Victor can wash pots.

Conditional

A compound statement formed by using the words "if… then" is denoted as p→q. which reads “if p, then q” or “p implies q”

In the case of p implies q, p is the assumption, and q is the conclusion.

p implies q is false only when a true assumption results in a false conclusion.

    For example:
  • p: It is gloomy outside.
  • q: I am emo.
  • p→q: If it is gloomy outside then I am emo.

Biconditional

A compound statement formed by combining p→q and q→p under conjunction.

Denoted as p⇔q, which reads “p if and only if q”

The biconditional is true when both p and q are true or false.

    For example:
  • p: I am rich
  • q: I have money
  • p⇔q: I am rich if and only if I have money.

Inverse Converse Contrapositive and Negation

A conditional is written as such p→q.

Inverse of a conditional is written as such ~p→~q.

Converse of a conditional s written as such q→p.

Contrapositive of a conditional is a mix of inverse and converse and is written as ~q→~p.

Contrapositive is logically equivalent to conditional.

Negation of conjunction p∧q is ~p⋁~q.

Negation of disjunction p⋁q is ~p∧~q.

Negation of conditional p→q is p∧~q.

Negation of biconditional p⇔q is (p⋀~q)⋁(q⋀~p).

You can find out whether or not each statement is true by making your truth tables.

With this knowledge, we’ll be able to use them in proofs by manipulating the original statements in the next chapter.

Type of Proofs

Direct Proof: proving something directly from known facts by simply stringing the known results together or performing correct mathematical manipulations to arrive at our conclusion

Proof by Counterexample: providing an example that shows the statement is false

Proof by Cases: examining multiple cases to show the statement is true in all possible scenarios

Proof by Contradiction/Indirect Proof: by proving the negation of the original statement to be false, it proves the original statement to be true
